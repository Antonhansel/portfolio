Working with remote developers has it's perks and is required for any company that has trouble finding great engineers at a fair price in a 25km radius.
A lot of product managers and tech leads prefer to work with on-site engineers because "it's easier" but for most projects and teams it just shows a lack of communication, organisation and delegation skills.

Let's get one thing out of the way, right now: Working with a remote or half team won't happen without efforts. Numbers vary on this but about 60% of communication goes through non-verbal cues. You're missing on all of those when working with a remote team and you'll need to compensate with over-communicating your intentions and thoughts.

In this article we will go through the myths about remote work and explore the methods and processes companies need to apply to work with remote developers, especially junior ones.
We will also expand on the three pillars of remote work:

- Trust
- Communication
- Hiring

## Trust your employees, leave your ego on the side

The number one complaint of managers against remote work is the lack of unplanned face-to-face interaction. With

It is true that unplanned face-to-face interactions is a great way to learn about project status and team mood informally. These interactions aren't possible in remote and a manager should set time aside for at least one meeting a week with each developers to talk about their work, how they feel in the team and the company, their involvment in the project, etc...

## Remote isn't for everyone

## Managers need to step up their game

Managers transitionning to remote need to learn new remote management skills and get specific training.
Remote requires self discipline, motivation and lets the true workers shine over slackers. It's harsh, but it's true both for developers and managers. As a manager, no more "making yourself look busy" by walking around open spaces with a folder under your arm and striking up conversations. You need to bring value to your team by solving problems ahead, making way for improvements and letting your developers focus by shielding them from always changing business needs.

As a developer if you are browsing Reddit instead of focusing on your tasks because nobody is watching over your shoulder, maybe remote work isn't for you. Focus, plow through your tasks of the day as fast as possible and you're then free to do anything you want.

## (Async) Communication

When working with a remote team, most communications will happens asynchronously across multiple time zones. Async communications means that people won't be getting back to you right away. The keys to async communication are:

- A clearly communicated purpose for the message and what you need out of it.
- Enough details included in the communication to address predictable follow-up questions the receiver might have.
- Provide supporting material if it’s task or project related.
- A known deadline for response.

Despite what you can think, async communication actually lead to more efficient teams. First, it enables people to work without interruptions. If you're currently working with an on-site team, how many times a day are you physically interrupted by a coworker for a non-urgent matter that could have been solved over slack ?

> “Asynchronous communication means I can take a step out for lunch and catch up on transcripts uninterrupted when I get back. Asynchronous communication means I can ask my coworker a question in-chat and not worry about bothering her since she’ll get back to me when she’s available. Asynchronous communication means I can go to rural Minnesota and feel like I’m working from the office like normal.”

## Always assume the best intentions

Working with remote developers means you'll be working with people coming from a vastly different background than yours, with different traditions, religion, customs and work ethic.

It is critical for you to understand that, regardless of your role, technical or not, your words can be interpreted differently by your team. [reword]

We, as humans and leaders, have been conditioned to be suspicious, to think that if something seems too good to be true, it probably is. As a hobbyist trader, it's actually vital to understand the real reasons, the "agenda" behind free advices, news and products.

It has always been very hard for me to CHOOSE to think differently and assume the best intentions.

I would argue that doing this in your life in general will greatly benefit your mental health and well being, but this is a subject for another article.

## Don't shoot yourself in the foot

Hiring is the biggest obstacle to building and scaling a company. The bottom line is that you need the best engineers for the job while maintaining your costs reasonably low. Having an anti-remote work culture will be detrimental in the long run.
Developers you need and can afford are likely to be located outside of commuting distance of your offices.
Great developers want better benefits. The data from Stack Overflow's developer Survey shows that more than a third of developers consider remote work the most important factors about picking a company, above languages, frameworks and company culture.
Allowing your employees to work remotely saves money on office space while making the company more competitive in the eyes of developers, win/win !
